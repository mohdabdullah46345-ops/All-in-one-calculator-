What is SIP? A Complete Beginner's Guide to Systematic Investment Plans

By Abdullah · Updated June 2026 · 6 min read

A Systematic Investment Plan, commonly known as SIP, is one of the most popular ways for ordinary people to invest in mutual funds. Instead of putting in a large lump sum at once, a SIP lets you invest a fixed amount at regular intervals — usually every month. This simple discipline has helped millions of investors build wealth steadily over time, without needing to time the market or follow it daily.

How does a SIP actually work?

When you start a SIP, you choose a mutual fund, an amount (for example ₹2,000 or ₹5,000), and a date each month. On that date, the amount is automatically deducted from your bank account and used to buy units of the fund at the current price, known as the Net Asset Value (NAV). When the market is down, your fixed amount buys more units; when the market is up, it buys fewer. Over time this evens out your average purchase price.

The two superpowers of SIP

1. Rupee-cost averaging

Because you invest the same amount regularly regardless of market conditions, you naturally buy more when prices are low and less when prices are high. This reduces the risk of investing everything at the "wrong" time and removes the emotional pressure of trying to predict short-term movements.

2. The power of compounding

Compounding means earning returns not just on your original investment, but also on the returns it has already generated. The longer you stay invested, the more dramatic this effect becomes. For example, a modest ₹5,000 monthly SIP at an assumed 12% annual return can grow into a substantial corpus over 15–20 years — and most of that final amount comes from returns, not your own contributions.

A simple example

Suppose you invest ₹5,000 every month for 10 years at an assumed annual return of 12%. Your total contribution would be ₹6,00,000. Thanks to compounding, the estimated maturity value could be around ₹11.6 lakh — meaning your money roughly doubled. Who should consider a SIP?

  • Salaried individuals who want to invest a part of their income automatically each month.
  • Beginners who feel nervous about investing a large amount all at once.
  • Long-term goal planners saving for retirement, a child's education, or a home.
  • Anyone who values discipline and wants to avoid the stress of market timing.

Things to keep in mind

SIPs invest in market-linked instruments, so returns are not guaranteed and can vary. The longer your investment horizon, the better SIPs tend to perform, because short-term ups and downs smooth out over time. It also helps to review your portfolio once a year and increase your SIP amount as your income grows — a habit sometimes called a "step-up SIP".

Frequently asked questions

Can I stop or pause my SIP?

Yes. SIPs are flexible. You can pause, stop, increase, or decrease them at any time without penalty, although staying invested for the long term usually gives the best results.

Is SIP the same as a mutual fund?

No. A mutual fund is the investment product, while a SIP is simply a method of investing into it regularly. You can also invest in the same fund as a one-time lump sum.
