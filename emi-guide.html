What is EMI? How Loan EMI is Calculated — A Complete Guide

By Abdullah · Updated June 2026 · 6 min read

EMI stands for Equated Monthly Installment. It is the fixed amount you pay to a bank or lender every month until your loan is fully repaid. Whether it is a home loan, car loan, personal loan, or education loan, the EMI bundles two things into one convenient payment: a part of the money you borrowed (the principal) and the interest charged on it.

Why is the EMI fixed every month?

One of the most useful features of an EMI is that it stays the same throughout the loan tenure (for a fixed-rate loan). This predictability makes budgeting easy — you know exactly how much will leave your account each month. However, the split between principal and interest changes over time. In the early months, a larger share goes towards interest; in the later months, more of it pays down the principal. This gradual shift is called amortization.

The EMI formula

Banks calculate EMI using a standard mathematical formula:

EMI = P × r × (1 + r)n ÷ ((1 + r)n − 1)

Here, P is the loan amount (principal), r is the monthly interest rate (annual rate divided by 12, then by 100), and n is the total number of monthly installments (years × 12). You do not need to calculate this by hand — our EMI Calculator does it instantly and also shows you the total interest you will pay over the life of the loan.

What affects your EMI?

  • Loan amount: A larger loan means a larger EMI.
  • Interest rate: Even a small change in the rate can significantly affect your total interest over a long tenure.
  • Tenure: A longer tenure lowers your monthly EMI but increases the total interest you pay. A shorter tenure does the opposite.

A quick example

Imagine a home loan of ₹10,00,000 at 9% annual interest for 20 years. The EMI works out to roughly ₹8,997 per month. Over 20 years you would pay about ₹11.6 lakh in interest alone — more than the amount you originally borrowed. This is exactly why understanding tenure and interest matters so much.

Smart tips to reduce your total interest

  • Choose a shorter tenure if you can afford a higher EMI — it dramatically cuts total interest.
  • Make prepayments whenever you have surplus funds; even one extra EMI per year shortens the loan.
  • Compare lenders and negotiate the interest rate before signing.
  • Maintain a good credit score to qualify for lower rates.
  • Consider a balance transfer if another lender offers a meaningfully lower rate.

Fixed vs floating interest rates

A fixed-rate loan keeps your EMI constant for the whole tenure, offering certainty. A floating-rate loan moves up or down with market rates, so your EMI (or tenure) may change over time. Floating rates are often lower to begin with but carry the risk of increasing later.

Frequently asked questions

Can my EMI change during the loan?

For fixed-rate loans, no. For floating-rate loans, the bank may revise either your EMI amount or the tenure when market rates change.

Is it better to choose a longer tenure for a smaller EMI?

A smaller EMI feels easier monthly, but you end up paying much more interest overall. Pick the shortest tenure your budget comfortably allows.
