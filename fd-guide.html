Fixed Deposit (FD) Explained: How FD Interest & Maturity Work

By Abdullah · Updated June 2026 · 5 min read

A Fixed Deposit, or FD, is one of the safest and most popular savings options offered by banks and post offices. You deposit a lump sum for a fixed period at a fixed interest rate, and in return the bank pays you guaranteed interest. Because the rate is locked in at the start, you know exactly how much your money will grow by the time the deposit matures.

Why people choose Fixed Deposits

  • Safety: Returns are guaranteed and do not depend on market movements.
  • Predictability: You know the maturity amount in advance.
  • Flexibility: Tenures range from a few days to several years.
  • Easy to open: Almost any bank account holder can start one in minutes.

How is FD interest calculated?

FD interest is usually calculated using compound interest, which means you earn interest on your interest. Most banks compound FD interest quarterly — that is, every three months the earned interest is added to your principal, and the next round of interest is calculated on this larger amount.

The compound interest formula is:

Maturity = P × (1 + r/n)(n × t)

Here P is your principal, r is the annual interest rate (as a decimal), n is the number of times interest is compounded per year (4 for quarterly), and t is the number of years. Our FD Calculator uses quarterly compounding to give you an accurate maturity estimate instantly.

Simple vs compound interest

With simple interest, you earn the same interest every year only on the original principal. With compound interest, your interest also earns interest, so the total grows faster the longer you stay invested. This is why an FD held for several years grows noticeably more than a short one at the same rate.

A quick example

Suppose you deposit ₹1,00,000 at 7% per year for 5 years, compounded quarterly. The estimated maturity value would be around ₹1,41,478 — meaning you earn roughly ₹41,478 in interest, more than you would with simple interest at the same rate.

Types of fixed deposits

  • Cumulative FD: Interest is compounded and paid at maturity — best for long-term growth.
  • Non-cumulative FD: Interest is paid out monthly, quarterly, or yearly — useful for regular income.
  • Tax-saving FD: A 5-year lock-in deposit that may offer tax benefits under applicable laws.
  • Senior citizen FD: Often offers a slightly higher interest rate for older depositors.

Tips to get the most from your FD

  • Compare rates across banks — small differences add up over long tenures.
  • Use laddering — split your money across FDs of different tenures so funds mature at different times.
  • Choose cumulative FDs if you do not need regular payouts, to benefit fully from compounding.
  • Avoid premature withdrawal when possible, as it usually reduces your interest.

Frequently asked questions

Is FD interest taxable?

Yes, interest earned on an FD is generally taxable as per your income slab. Tax rules vary, so check the current regulations or consult a tax professional.

Can I break an FD before maturity?

Yes, most FDs allow premature withdrawal, but banks usually charge a small penalty and pay a slightly lower interest rate.
